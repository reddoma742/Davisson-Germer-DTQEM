\title{Microscopic Derivation of Joint-Bath Decoherence: Cross-Spectral Density and Inter-Bath Correlation in Coupled Bosonic Reservoirs}

\author{Reddouane Berramdane}
\email{reddoma@gmail.com}
\affiliation{Independent Researcher, Oujda, Morocco}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
In a companion study (Paper I), a phenomenological model for joint-bath quantum decoherence was introduced, featuring a bilinear cross-coupling term $c$ to capture the synergistic action of correlated environmental modes. However, the microscopic origin of this term was left open. In this work, we provide a first-principles derivation using a generalized Caldeira--Leggett model with mutually coupled bosonic baths. By applying a canonical transformation to mass-weighted coordinates, we decouple the system into independent normal modes, yielding an explicit analytical expression for the cross-spectral density $J_{12}(\omega)$. We show that the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality provides a fundamental bound on the normalized cross-correlation coefficient, ensuring $|\rho(\omega)| \leq 1$. Numerical validation on a minimal two-mode Ohmic--Drude model indicates that the phenomenological value $\rho \approx 0.855$ (extracted in Paper I) is consistent with a physically realistic, weak-to-moderate inter-bath coupling ($\lambda_{\text{opt}} \approx 0.095$). Finally, we demonstrate that the bilinear form $c \cdot u \cdot v$ serves as a consistent local approximation of the exact microscopic $\sqrt{uv}$ dependence, consistent with physical boundary conditions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Quantum decoherence in open systems is traditionally modeled by assuming that distinct environmental channels---such as which-path information leakage and thermal dephasing---act independently \cite{Breuer2002, Weiss2012}. While this independent-bath assumption simplifies the Feynman--Vernon influence functional \cite{Feynman1963}, it may fail to capture the physics of platforms where a shared phonon or photon bath mediates multiple decoherence pathways simultaneously \cite{Hornberger2003}. 

In a recent phenomenological study (hereafter referred to as Paper I) \cite{Berramdane2026}, we proposed a minimal extension to the standard decoherence model by introducing a bilinear cross-coupling term $c I_{\text{path}} \Delta T / T_{\text{ref}}$. Calibration against synthetic and experimental molecule-interferometry data yielded an effective inter-bath correlation coefficient $\rho \approx 0.855$. However, Paper I explicitly deferred the microscopic derivation of this term, treating $c$ as an empirical fit parameter.

This work addresses this gap. We present a microscopic derivation of the cross-correlation kernel starting from a Hamiltonian with explicit inter-bath coupling. We demonstrate how canonical transformations naturally generate a cross-spectral density $J_{12}(\omega)$, show that it is constrained by the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality, and numerically validate that the value $\rho \approx 0.855$ is consistent with realistic, weak-to-moderate physical coupling between environmental modes.

\section{Hamiltonian and Normal Mode Transformation}
We consider a quantum system $S$ coupled to two bosonic baths, $B_1$ and $B_2$, which are themselves mutually coupled. The total Hamiltonian is given by the generalized Caldeira--Leggett model \cite{Caldeira1983}:
\begin{equation}
\hat{H}_{\text{total}} = \hat{H}_S + \hat{H}_{B_1} + \hat{H}_{B_2} + \hat{H}_{SB_1} + \hat{H}_{SB_2} + \hat{H}_{B_1 B_2},
\label{eq:H_total}
\end{equation}
where the bath Hamiltonians and their mutual coupling are:
\begin{align}
\hat{H}_{B_i} &= \sum_{k} \left( \frac{\hat{p}_{k,i}^2}{2m_{k,i}} + \frac{1}{2} m_{k,i} \omega_{k,i}^2 \hat{q}_{k,i}^2 \right), \quad i \in \{1, 2\}, \\
\hat{H}_{B_1 B_2} &= \sum_{k,j} \lambda_{kj} \hat{q}_{k,1} \hat{q}_{j,2}.
\label{eq:H_bath_coupling}
\end{align}
To decouple the kinetic energy, we introduce mass-weighted coordinates and momenta: $\tilde{\mathbf{Q}} = \mathbf{M}^{1/2} \mathbf{Q}$ and $\tilde{\mathbf{P}} = \mathbf{M}^{-1/2} \mathbf{P}$, where $\mathbf{M}$ is the block-diagonal mass matrix. The bath Hamiltonian then takes the compact form:
\begin{equation}
\hat{H}_{\text{baths}} = \frac{1}{2} \tilde{\mathbf{P}}^T \tilde{\mathbf{P}} + \frac{1}{2} \tilde{\mathbf{Q}}^T \tilde{\mathbf{K}} \tilde{\mathbf{Q}},
\label{eq:H_mass_weighted}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\mathbf{K}} = \mathbf{M}^{-1/2} \mathbf{K} \mathbf{M}^{-1/2}$ is the symmetric, mass-weighted stiffness matrix containing the scaled coupling elements $\tilde{\lambda}_{kj} = \lambda_{kj} / \sqrt{m_{k,1} m_{j,2}}$.

Since $\tilde{\mathbf{K}}$ is a real symmetric matrix, it can be diagonalized by an orthogonal matrix $\mathbf{O}$ ($\mathbf{O}^T \mathbf{O} = \mathbf{I}$):
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{O}^T \tilde{\mathbf{K}} \mathbf{O} = \mathbf{\Omega}_{\text{diag}}^2 = \text{diag}(\tilde{\omega}_1^2, \tilde{\omega}_2^2, \dots, \tilde{\omega}_N^2),
\label{eq:diagonalization}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\omega}_\alpha$ are the eigenfrequencies of the \textit{normal modes}. We define the normal mode coordinates $\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{O}^T \tilde{\mathbf{Q}}$, which constitutes a canonical transformation. The bath Hamiltonian is now fully decoupled:
\begin{equation}
\hat{H}_{\text{baths}} = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} \left( \frac{\hat{P}_{X,\alpha}^2}{2} + \frac{1}{2} \tilde{\omega}_\alpha^2 \hat{X}_\alpha^2 \right).
\label{eq:H_normal_modes}
\end{equation}
The original system-bath coupling $\hat{H}_{SB} = \hat{X}_S \otimes \mathbf{g}^T \mathbf{Q}$ transforms to $\hat{H}_{SB} = \hat{X}_S \otimes \sum_\alpha \tilde{g}_\alpha \hat{X}_\alpha$, where the effective coupling to the $\alpha$-th normal mode is $\tilde{g}_\alpha = (\mathbf{O}^T \mathbf{M}^{-1/2} \mathbf{g})_\alpha$. 

To isolate the contributions of each original bath, we partition the orthogonal matrix $\mathbf{O}$ into blocks:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{O} = \left(\begin{array}{cc} \mathbf{O}_{11} & \mathbf{O}_{12} \\ \mathbf{O}_{21} & \mathbf{O}_{22} \end{array}\right).
\label{eq:O_partition}
\end{equation}
The effective coupling splits as $\tilde{g}_\alpha = \tilde{g}_{1,\alpha} + \tilde{g}_{2,\alpha}$, where:
\begin{align}
\tilde{g}_{1,\alpha} &= \sum_{k} (\mathbf{O}_{11})_{k\alpha} \frac{g_{k,1}}{\sqrt{m_{k,1}}}, \quad
\tilde{g}_{2,\alpha} = \sum_{j} (\mathbf{O}_{21})_{j\alpha} \frac{g_{j,2}}{\sqrt{m_{j,2}}}.
\label{eq:g_split}
\end{align}

\section{Cross-Spectral Density and Physical Bounds}
The total spectral density of the decoupled normal modes is $J_{\text{total}}(\omega) = \frac{\pi}{2} \sum_\alpha \frac{\tilde{g}_\alpha^2}{\tilde{\omega}_\alpha} \delta(\omega - \tilde{\omega}_\alpha)$. Expanding $\tilde{g}_\alpha^2 = (\tilde{g}_{1,\alpha} + \tilde{g}_{2,\alpha})^2$, we naturally decompose the total spectral density into three components:
\begin{equation}
J_{\text{total}}(\omega) = J_{11}(\omega) + J_{22}(\omega) + 2 J_{12}(\omega),
\label{eq:J_total_split}
\end{equation}
where the \textbf{cross-spectral density} $J_{12}(\omega)$ is defined explicitly as:
\begin{equation}
J_{12}(\omega) = \frac{\pi}{2} \sum_{\alpha} \frac{\tilde{g}_{1,\alpha} \tilde{g}_{2,\alpha}}{\tilde{\omega}_\alpha} \delta(\omega - \tilde{\omega}_\alpha).
\label{eq:J12_explicit}
\end{equation}
If $\lambda_{kj} = 0$, the matrix $\mathbf{O}$ is block-diagonal, forcing either $\mathbf{O}_{11}$ or $\mathbf{O}_{21}$ to yield zero overlap for any given mode, resulting in $J_{12}(\omega) = 0$. Thus, $J_{12}(\omega) \neq 0$ serves as the mathematical signature of bath entanglement via $\hat{H}_{B_1 B_2}$.

From the explicit definition in Eq.~\eqref{eq:J12_explicit}, the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality for sums guarantees that for any frequency $\omega$:
\begin{equation}
|J_{12}(\omega)|^2 \leq J_{11}(\omega) J_{22}(\omega).
\label{eq:cauchy_schwarz}
\end{equation}
This fundamental bound allows us to define a normalized, frequency-dependent cross-correlation coefficient:
\begin{equation}
\rho(\omega) = \frac{J_{12}(\omega)}{\sqrt{J_{11}(\omega) J_{22}(\omega)}}, \quad \text{with} \quad |\rho(\omega)| \leq 1.
\label{eq:rho_def}
\end{equation}

\section{Numerical Validation and Connection to Phenomenology}
In Paper I, a phenomenological analysis of the decoherence exponent yielded an effective inter-bath correlation coefficient $\rho \approx 0.855$. To validate that this value is physically attainable, we constructed a minimal numerical model consisting of two Ohmic--Drude baths with $N_1 = N_2 = 2$ modes each. We performed a parameter sweep over the coupling strength $\lambda$ (where $\lambda_{kj} = \lambda \sqrt{\omega_{k,1} \omega_{j,2}}$). 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\columnwidth]{figures/cauchy_schwarz_lambda_sweep.png}
\caption{Parameter sweep over inter-bath coupling $\lambda$. The peak correlation $\rho_{\text{peak}}$ monotonically increases while strictly respecting the Cauchy--Schwarz bound ($\rho \leq 1$). The optimal coupling $\lambda_{\text{opt}} \approx 0.095$ yields $\rho_{\text{peak}} \approx 0.855$, matching the phenomenological extraction from Paper I.}
\label{fig:lambda_sweep}
\end{figure}

The numerical diagonalization reveals that as $\lambda$ increases, $\rho_{\text{peak}} = \max_\omega |\rho(\omega)|$ monotonically increases while respecting the bound in Eq.~\eqref{eq:cauchy_schwarz} (see Fig.~\ref{fig:lambda_sweep}). We find that an optimal coupling strength of $\lambda_{\text{opt}} \approx 0.095$ yields a peak correlation of $\rho_{\text{peak}} \approx 0.855$. 

This numerical result corresponds to a weak-to-moderate inter-bath coupling, with $\|\hat{H}_{B_1B_2}\|/\|\hat{H}_{B_i}\| \lesssim 12\%$, which justifies the microscopic derivation via canonical transformation without requiring a full non-perturbative treatment. It indicates that the correlation $\rho \approx 0.855$ extracted from experimental data fitting in Paper I is consistent with a realistic physical coupling between the environmental modes.

\textbf{Bridging to the Bilinear Model:} The exact second-order cumulant expansion of the influence functional yields a cross-term proportional to $\sqrt{u v}$, where $u \propto I_{\text{path}}$ and $v \propto \Delta T/T_{\text{ref}}$. The bilinear form $c \cdot u \cdot v$ utilized in Paper I is therefore a local approximation of this exact microscopic result. Specifically, expanding $f(u, v) = \sqrt{uv}$ in a Taylor series around a nominal operating point $(u_0, v_0)$, the mixed second-order partial derivative is $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial u \partial v} = \frac{1}{4\sqrt{u_0 v_0}}$. Including the $1/2!$ factor from the Taylor series, the leading-order cross term becomes:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\times} = \frac{(u - u_0)(v - v_0)}{8\sqrt{u_0 v_0}}.
\label{eq:taylor_cross}
\end{equation}
Matching the coefficient of $uv$ at leading order to the phenomenological parameter $c$ provides a consistent mapping:
\begin{equation}
c = \frac{\rho \sqrt{ab}}{4\sqrt{u_0 v_0}}.
\label{eq:c_rho_relation}
\end{equation}
Using the calibrated values from Paper I ($c=0.5$, $u_0=v_0=0.5$, $a=1.6968$, $b=0.8055$), Eq.~\eqref{eq:c_rho_relation} independently yields $\rho \approx 0.855$, providing a consistent mapping between the microscopic derivation and the phenomenological model.

\section{Conclusion}
We have provided a microscopic derivation of the joint-bath decoherence cross-coupling term. By applying a canonical transformation to a generalized Caldeira--Leggett model, we derived an explicit expression for the cross-spectral density $J_{12}(\omega)$ and showed that it is constrained by the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality. Numerical validation indicates that the phenomenological correlation coefficient $\rho \approx 0.855$ is consistent with a realistic, weak-to-moderate inter-bath coupling ($\lambda_{\text{opt}} \approx 0.095$). Furthermore, we demonstrated that the bilinear form $c \cdot u \cdot v$ serves as a consistent local approximation of the exact $\sqrt{uv}$ dependence, satisfying all physical boundary conditions. Future work will extend this framework to non-Markovian regimes and specific solid-state platforms, such as superconducting qubits.
